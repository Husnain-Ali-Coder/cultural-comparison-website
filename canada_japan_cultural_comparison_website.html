Doing Business in Japan

A cultural comparison guide for Canadian businesses expanding into the Japanese market

Introduction & Learning Goal

The purpose of this website is to highlight and explain the impact of culture on business between Canada and Japan. Understanding cultural differences helps Canadian professionals avoid misunderstandings and build strong, respectful business relationships.

Why Trade Between Canada & Japan Matters

  • Trade Relationship Began: Canada and Japan began trading in the late 1800s, with formal relations established in 1929.
  • Annual Trade Value: Over $30 billion CAD annually.
  • Key Canadian Exports: Natural resources, agriculture (wheat, canola), seafood, lumber.
  • Key Japanese Exports: Vehicles, electronics, machinery, technology.
  • Trade Agreement: Comprehensive and Progressive Agreement for Trans-Pacific Partnership (CPTPP).

Five Key Cultural Differences

1. Communication Style

Canada: Direct and open communication is valued.

Japan: Indirect communication is common. Silence and context are important.

Business Impact: Canadians must read between the lines and avoid pressuring for immediate answers.

2. Decision-Making

Canada: Decisions are often made quickly by managers.

Japan: Decisions are made through group consensus.

Business Impact: Expect longer timelines and multiple meetings.

3. Hierarchy

Canada: Relatively flat organizational structures.

Japan: Strong respect for hierarchy and seniority.

Business Impact: Address senior leaders first and show respect to rank.

4. Business Etiquette

Canada: Handshakes and casual professionalism.

Japan: Bowing, formal greetings, and exchanging business cards with care.

Business Impact: Mishandling a business card can be seen as disrespectful.

5. Work Culture

Canada: Emphasis on work-life balance.

Japan: Long working hours and strong company loyalty.

Business Impact: Commitment and dedication are highly valued.

Tips for Canadian Business Travelers

  • Be punctual—arriving late is considered disrespectful.
  • Dress conservatively and professionally.
  • Learn basic Japanese greetings.
  • Avoid aggressive negotiation tactics.
  • Show patience and respect during meetings.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Interrupting or speaking too directly.
  • Ignoring hierarchy or seniority.
  • Expecting immediate decisions.
  • Being overly informal too quickly.
